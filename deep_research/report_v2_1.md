# Expansion Index: Human-AI Coevolution Papers — 45 New Entries (2022–2026)

## Section 1 — Calibration check

The four-phase framework treats AI use as a coevolutionary process in which the human-side capability required for safe and productive use shifts as delegation deepens: from **critical thinking** (Phase 1, AI as Tool) → **evaluative expertise** (Phase 2, AI as Assistant) → **metacognitive monitoring** (Phase 3, AI as Executor) → **systems thinking** (Phase 4, AI as Organization). An in-scope paper must do at least one of: (a) document a human capability that AI use empowers or weakens at one of these phases; (b) study how human feedback during use reshapes the model and then reshapes future human behaviour through the sycophancy / mode-collapse / format-optimization lens; or (c) take a position or survey stance on how humans should evolve alongside AI. "Filling a gap" here means strengthening Phase 3 (executor oversight, vigilance, deskilling), Phase 4 (multi-agent governance, AI-mediated research, constitutional/policy frameworks), and widening domain coverage beyond software engineering and chatbot writing — to medicine, law, customer service, voice/negotiation, education with empirical depth, and cross-cultural settings. Every entry below has been verified against an authoritative source page (arXiv abs, ACM DL DOI, Lancet, JAMA, Nature, npj Digital Medicine, Springer, OpenReview, SSRN, METR, Microsoft Research, PLOS, PMC, Brookings, OpenAI publication page). All entries are confirmed not to duplicate the supplied 110-entry list (matched by title and arXiv ID).

- **Date (v1 / earliest public release):** 2025-01 (CHI 2025 camera-ready); conference 2025-04
- **Publisher / venue:** CHI 2025 (ACM)
- **Phase tag:** phase-1
- **Secondary themes:** LH, PS
- **Keywords:** critical thinking, knowledge workers, GenAI confidence, cognitive offloading, task stewardship
- **Primary link:** https://doi.org/10.1145/3706598.3713778
- **arXiv ID:** —
- **Other source links:**

- **TLDR:** Survey of 319 knowledge workers across 936 first-hand GenAI work-task examples. ## Section 4 — Coverage gaps that remain

- **Non-Anglo, non-OECD cross-cultural longitudinal studies** of AI adoption (Africa, Latin America, MENA, rural Asia) remain very sparse in indexable peer-reviewed venues; the Alibaba customer-service field experiment is the closest hit, but its cross-cultural framing is implicit, not foregrounded.
- **Finance professionals (traders, analysts, compliance officers)** longitudinal use studies remain thin in this expansion pass; the only finance-specific paper in the index is the bibliometric survey already at #2 of the existing 110.
- **Public-sector / civil-service AI-adoption longitudinal studies** are limited to method-application papers like LLM-MAC; no verifiable empirical capability-change study at the named level required here.
- **Voice and video AI-mediated communication beyond negotiation** is thin. The Trucey study (entry 7) is the strongest single-domain anchor, but verifiable longitudinal speech-act, video-call summarisation or video-mediated mentoring studies remain elusive.
- **Mediation and dispute-resolution AI-mediated studies with measurable outcomes** are sparse; the closest hits are non-peer-reviewed industry pilots.
- **Real-team Devin / Cursor / Claude Code field studies** with longitudinal outcomes are mostly commentary; the METR RCT (entry 16) is the closest empirical anchor and is itself a short-term RCT, not a multi-month team study.
- **Third-party empirical audits of constitutional-AI-trained models** against community values are scarce — the Anthropic/CIP CCAI paper (entry 25) is the producer-side evidence; external audits are missing.
- **Educator-side longitudinal studies** (teachers' practice change) are weak; student-side coverage is far stronger.
- **Cross-domain comparison studies** (e.g., does the deskilling pattern observed in colonoscopy generalise to ECG interpretation, dermatology, mammography?) are mostly commentary in Lancet/JAMA opinion pieces rather than primary research yet.

These coverage gaps suggest future expansion passes should explicitly seek: (i) primary studies in finance and public administration, (ii) longitudinal Devin/Claude-Code field studies in real product teams as those tools mature, (iii) cross-cultural replications of the Lee et al. CHI 2025 critical-thinking survey, and (iv) ethnographic studies of educators and clinicians adapting institutional practice to AI.
