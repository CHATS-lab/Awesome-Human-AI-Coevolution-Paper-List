Expanding the Human–AI Coevolution Index
Executive summary
The uploaded brief resolves the otherwise unspecified topic: this report curates additional papers for a structured academic index on how humans must evolve to use AI well as AI advances. The intended audience, deadline, and citation style remain unspecified in the brief, so I optimized for high-confidence academic additions, analytical coverage of underweighted phases, and export-friendly metadata rather than house-style formatting. 

The strongest additions cluster in five places. First, classic human-factors papers sharpen the index’s conceptual backbone around use, misuse, disuse, abuse, trust calibration, and levels of automation. Second, newer empirical work in medicine, public administration, finance, and education directly tests when people over-rely on AI, when they resist it, and how performance changes after assistance is introduced. Third, several 2024–2026 studies fill the especially underweighted phase-3 space: human monitoring of systems that draft, plan, or execute substantial workflow chunks while humans supervise, override, or repair. Fourth, a smaller but strategically important set of papers fills the emerging-phase-4 / phase-4 gap around public-input alignment, multi-agent governance, and post-deployment accountability. Fifth, the newer education papers help separate “AI helps performance now” from “AI builds or weakens durable capability later,” which is central to the index’s thesis. 

I recommend importing the 32 additions below in this order of urgency: phase-3 and phase-4 papers first, then framework/foundational papers, then education and sector-specific trust-calibration studies. That order best improves the current list’s balance by adding material on supervisory control, oversight under partial autonomy, public-sector hybrid decisions, and social/governance mechanisms for future agentic systems, which are precisely the areas that are least saturated in the existing index. 
 

Calibration and methodology
The brief asked for additions that are high quality, not already in the existing 110-paper list, and especially useful for underweighted phases. I therefore prioritized papers that met four filters at once: direct relevance to human capability under AI assistance, clear empirical or review value, official or primary source pages, and novelty relative to the existing list. I treated publisher pages, official repository pages, and canonical research pages as the source of truth in roughly this order: publisher/conference page first, then arXiv/OpenReview/PubMed/SSRN/NBER/Open-access institutional record only when needed to recover a date or affiliation that the publisher page did not expose.

Recommendations and remaining gaps
The highest-value next step for the index is to rebalance the center of gravity away from “AI as answer engine” and toward “AI as supervised collaborator, drafter, executor, and governable collective.” In practical terms, that means importing the phase-3 and phase-4 additions first, because they best capture the transition from prompting to supervising systems that make larger workflow contributions and from local interaction design to social, organizational, and civic control layers. 

A second recommendation is structural: maintain a consistent distinction between capability gain, capability substitution, and capability atrophy risk. The education papers in this batch are especially useful because they show all three patterns can coexist: some interventions improve self-regulation and sense-making, while others surface dependency, misuse, or uncritical acceptance. That distinction should become a first-class indexing dimension, not just an interpretive afterthought. 

The main remaining gaps are clear. Relative to the selected evidence, there are still too few high-confidence studies on voice/video/negotiation mediation, longitudinal skill retention in legal and financial practice, real-team studies of autonomous coding/agentic workflows, and cross-cultural work outside the most common North American and European publication streams. That is an inference from the reviewed source mix rather than a formal bibliometric count, but it is robust enough to guide the next search round. Several exact-day publication dates and some full institutional rosters were also not exposed on the accessible official pages, so I preserved only verified precision rather than guessing.
