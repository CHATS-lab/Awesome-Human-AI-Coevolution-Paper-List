The Dynamics of Human-AI Coevolution: A Structured Repository of Bidirectional Influence and Adaptation
Section 1 — Calibration check
The following repository centers on the phenomenon of human-AI coevolution, defined as the recursive and temporal process where human behavior, cognition, and norms are reshaped by interaction with artificial intelligence, while the AI system is simultaneously reshaped by human inputs, preferences, and deployment contexts. Inclusion is strictly limited to research that documents this bidirectional causality; studies focusing exclusively on model performance (Human → AI) or one-off user experience effects (AI → Human) without a longitudinal or training-loop component are excluded. The primary filter applied ensures that each entry identifies a specific mechanism of human-to-AI influence—such as Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), prompting strategies, or demonstration data—alongside a measurable AI-to-human effect, including skill atrophy, language homogenization, or mental model shifting.   

Section 2 — Paper entries
The entries below are grouped by their primary taxonomic category. Where a paper addresses multiple categories, it is cross-referenced within the relevant narrative descriptions.

Section 3 — Borderline cases
The following candidates were considered for the repository but ultimately excluded to maintain the rigor of the bidirectional coevolutionary filter.

Jimenez et al., SWE-bench: Can language models resolve real-world github issues? (2024): Excluded because it is a pure capability benchmark (AI only) with no temporal or human-interaction component.   

Taori and Hashimoto, Model Collapse on Synthetic Data (2023): Excluded as it focuses on AI-on-AI dynamics (synthetic data feedback) with no direct human-in-the-loop coevolutionary measurement.   

Hancock et al., AI-Mediated Communication (2020): Excluded because it focuses on how AI-generated messages between two humans change their relationship, rather than a recursive human-AI training/adaptation loop.   

Ziegler et al., GitHub Copilot Productivity Study (2022): Excluded as a one-shot productivity measurement study with no analysis of long-term skill shift or model adaptation.   

Rafailov et al., Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (2023): Excluded as a core machine learning method paper; only in-scope if discussed through the societal lens of preference-feedback loops.   

Barke et al., Grounded Copilot usage patterns (2023): Excluded because it is a short-term observational study of developer behavior without a training-loop or longitudinal skill decay analysis.   

Section 4 — Coverage gaps
During the research process, several gaps were identified in the available literature, particularly regarding the intersection of specific domains and coevolutionary dynamics:

Scientific Practice Coevolution: While papers like Zuccarelli et al.  touch on academic writing, there is a lack of longitudinal studies on how AI discovery agents (like those for materials science) reshape the scientific method or hypothesis-generation strategies of human researchers over years.   

Long-term Physical Co-adaptation: Most longitudinal studies focus on digital interfaces (writing, coding). There is sparse high-quality research on how humans and embodied robots co-adapt their physical environments or sensorimotor strategies over multi-month home deployments.   

Educational Equity Feedbacks: While Marcolino et al.  provide a 5-year perspective, there is a lack of research on the "feedback loop of inequality"—how differential access to high-quality human-AI coevolutionary environments creates widening achievement gaps over a decade.

"co-evolution of scientific hypothesis generation with AI discovery agents"

"long-term effects of AI-assisted surgery on surgical dexterity" (yielded only short-term simulators).

"recursive feedback loops in AI-assisted legal reasoning" (yielded primarily position papers rather than longitudinal data).
